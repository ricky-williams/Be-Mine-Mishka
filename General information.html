WHAT IS AUTISM?


Autism, or autism spectrum disorder (ASD) can be defined as a rang of complicated conditions characterized by challenges with social, repetitive behaviors, speech and nonverbal communication. ASD can be a small problem or a disability that needs full-time care in a special facility. People with autism find it hard to communicate with others. They also find it hard to understand what other people think and feel. This then makes it hard for them to express themselves, either with words, or through gestures, facial expressions, and touch. People with ASD might have problems with learning as well (Autism speak, 2020).

SYMPTOMS OF AUTISM


Symptoms of ASD typically show clearly during early childhood, between 12 to 24 months old, however symptoms may appear later. Here are symptoms if you suspect your child has autism.


The core symptoms of autism are:

social communication challenges and restricted, repetitive behaviors.

Other signs/symptoms:

Abnormal Body Posturing or Facial Expressions, Abnormal Tone of Voice, Avoidance of Eye Contact or Poor Eye Contact, Behavioral Disturbances, Deficits in Language Comprehension, Delay in Learning to Speak, Flat or Monotonous Speech, Inappropriate Social Interaction, Intense Focus on One Topic, Lack of Empathy, Lack of Understanding Social Cues, Learning Disability or Difficulty, Not Engaging in Play With Peers, Preoccupation With Specific Topics, Problems With Two-Way Conversation, Repeating Words or Phrases, Repetitive Movements, Self-Abusive Behaviors, Sleep Disturbances, Social Withdrawal, Unusual Reactions in Social Settings, Using Odd Words or Phrases,(MedicineNet, 2021).

HOW IS AUTISM DIAGNOSED?


It is not easy to make an ASD diagnosis, there is not lab test for it, so doctors have to rely on observing the behaviors of very young children as well as listening to their parents concerns(cdc.gov, 2020).
Getting an autism diagnosis is a two stage process and it starts with your pediatrician.

Developmental screening
Pediatricians are the first step, every child gets an assessment at their 18-24 month check-ups to make sure they are on track and don't have an symptoms. Pediatricians will ask parents about their family history, if anyone has ASD in their family and about their child's behavior. The doctor will look at these milestones such as, did the baby smile by 6 months, did they baby mimic sounds or facial expressions by 9 months, and was the baby babbling and cooing by 12 months (cdc.gov, 2020). Doctors will also ask a rang of other questions which can be found HERE.

Your responds to the questions asked are very important in your child's screening. If it checks out and there is no concerns then that will be the end of it. But if there is development issues and the doctor has concerns, they will refer you to a specialist for more tests.

if your child requires more test you next appointment will be with a team of ASD specialists child psychologist, speech-language pathologist, and occupational therapist. You may also meet with a developmental pediatrician and a neurologist (cdc.gov, 2020).
This evaluation is usually to check things like your child’s cognitive level, language abilities, and other life skills like eating, dressing themselves, and going to the bathroom. For an official diagnosis, your child must meet the standards of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5).

If your child shows potential symptoms of ASD, their doctor may monitor their development with a new device that uses data and artificial intelligence to help diagnose ASD. The Cognoa ASD Diagnosis Aid is a machine learning–based software that follows children between the ages of 18 months and 5 years and helps evaluate and identify any developmental issues (cdc.gov, 2020).
